# Decision-Making by Consensus Doesn’t Work in the AI Era

*Jonathan Rosenthal & Neal Zuckerman • Harvard Business Review, April 2026*

## Core Argument

AI is bringing about an organisational reckoning. The companies that survive will not be those with the best algorithms or the most data — they will be those with the courage to abandon consensus-based decision-making, the dominant management principle of the past fifty years.

**Central thesis:** Consensus management was a rational adaptation to a pre-digital world of geographic breadth and asynchronous communication. It is now a critical liability because AI has made both of its core weaknesses — speed and information distortion — fatal.

---

## Why Consensus Management Fails in the AI Era

### Weakness 1: Speed

Decisions route through lawyers, marketing, PR, investor relations, risk managers, and compliance. Each layer smooths audacious initiatives, slows reaction time, and optimises for defensibility rather than speed. Consensus culture is collegial, risk-averse, and built for stability — not velocity.

### Weakness 2: Information Distortion

At each layer of consensus process, gatekeepers filter and interpret information. Signals degrade. By the time intelligence reaches the C-suite, it has been curated, smoothed, and stripped of the weak signals that often harbour the most critical clues.

The result is what the authors call **"Success Theater"**: weekly dashboards and siloed reports crafted by middle managers whose careers depend on protecting the status quo.

AI compounds both failures:

- It accelerates the speed at which companies must operate, making slowness more dangerous.
- As AI accelerates decision cycles, working from filtered information becomes an even greater liability.

The result is an organisation that is both **slow and blind**.

## Two Structural Changes

### 1. The Autonomous Scrum

Most organisations give small teams permission to recommend. High-velocity organisations give them permission to act.

Scrums of six to eight people — interdisciplinary, empowered, unshackled by bureaucratic approval chains — should become the organisational default, not the exception.

This departs from traditional agile in one critical respect:

| Team Structure | Decision Authority |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Traditional Scrum | Recommends and escalates |
| Autonomous Scrum | Owns outcomes |

Every layer of approval is a form of impedance. Leadership’s role shifts to providing the data environment and tools in which scrums can make good decisions, and requiring a time-bound, evidence-backed case for any veto.

The United Airlines 2002–2006 bankruptcy restructuring — one of the largest corporate reorganisations in US history — was run on exactly this model. Six cross-disciplinary working groups of six to ten people, each with substantial latitude, each with the default presumption that their recommendations would be adopted.

The structure produced the merger with Continental Airlines, an outcome impossible under traditional management.

#### Role of AI

AI agents equip the Autonomous Scrum with:

- Deep subject matter expertise
- Near-instantaneous feedback on outcomes
- Continuous analysis without coordination overhead

In effect, AI provides **an army of savants without adding more humans**.

---

### 2. The OVIS Framework for Decision Rights

When decisions result from consensus, accountability is elusive. OVIS is its deliberate replacement.

| Role | Responsibility |
|--------|---------------|
| **O — Owner** | One person owns the decision and is accountable for the outcome. |
| **V — Veto** | One or two people who can formally block a choice. |
| **I — Influence** | Those whose input the Owner must consider but whose approval is not required. |
| **S — Support** | Everyone else supports the outcome regardless of personal preference. |

The distinction between **Veto** and **Influence** is critical — conflating the two recreates the consensus problem OVIS is designed to solve.

The Owner is accountable for the outcome. That accountability is what makes the system work.

#### The Bezos Principle

> "Disagree and Commit."

OVIS gives structure to the disagreeing, then hands decision power to the Owner, compelling everyone else to commit.

AI marshals information, simulates outcomes, and challenges assumptions — but a human must remain in the loop.

---

## What Boards Must Demand

Transformation is unlikely without a board of true believers.

Boards operating off filtered, committee-approved C-suite reports are not exercising governance — they are perpetuating the distortion field.

### Fiduciary Duty in the AI Era Requires

- Unfiltered access to real-time signals that bypass executive summaries.
- Short, bounded experiments with clear success metrics and accountability.
- Reserving full board deliberation for:
  - Major acquisitions
  - Culture-defining hires
  - Ethical dilemmas
- Asking the survival question directly:

> Does leadership have the disposition to decide at this pace?

---

## The Leadership Disposition Required

The executives who thrive will share a particular disposition:

- Comfortable — even excited — making consequential decisions on incomplete information.
- Trusting signals over instinct.
- Preferring speed over process.
- Preferring small teams over consensus.
- Demanding continuous feedback loops.

They recognise that the greatest sin is not making the wrong call but making **no call at all**.

### Authors’ Conclusion

The Great AI Reorganisation will not be won by companies with:

- The most compute
- The deepest pockets

It will be won by those with:

- The clarity to see what the new environment demands
- The courage to build it

This is ultimately a test of **character rather than capability or intellect**.
